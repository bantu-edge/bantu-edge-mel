What standard MEL misses

Six gaps in conventional frameworks

After reviewing dozens of MEL frameworks used across Southern Africa and international development, these are the consistent gaps — and the starting point for how Bantu-Edge works differently.

Communities are treated as data sources, not decision-makers
Standard MEL extracts information from communities but rarely returns it in usable form, or builds community agency into the design of the system itself.
History and power are treated as background, not as data
Structural inequality, colonial legacies, and power dynamics are not context notes — they actively shape what change is possible and what evidence means.
Gender and inclusion are a column, not a lens
Disaggregating by sex is not a GESI framework. Genuine inclusion requires rethinking who designs the system, what gets measured, and how findings are interpreted.
Accountability and learning are assumed to be the same thing
Reporting for compliance and learning for improvement require different data, different relationships, and different psychological conditions. Conflating them damages both.
Reports are built to be filed, not to be used
Most MEL products are designed for submission, not for decision-making. The question of who will read this and what they will do with it rarely shapes the design.
Uncomfortable findings get smoothed over
MEL systems that only surface positive findings are not trustworthy learning systems. Structural incentives routinely distort what programmes report about themselves.
