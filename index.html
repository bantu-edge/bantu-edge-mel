What standard MEL misses

Six gaps in conventional frameworks

After reviewing dozens of MEL frameworks used across Southern Africa and international development, these are the consistent gaps — and the starting point for how Bantu-Edge works differently.

Communities are treated as data sources, not decision-makers
Communities appear as survey respondents and focus group participants. They are rarely involved in designing what gets measured, interpreting what findings mean, or deciding what happens next.
History and power are treated as background, not as data
Inequality, historical exclusion, and governance complexity get noted in narrative sections — then left out of the actual analysis. Standard logframes have no field for why things are the way they are.
Gender and inclusion are a column, not a lens
GESI appears as a disaggregation category or a standalone indicator. It rarely shapes how the entire MEL system is designed — who participates, what gets measured, how findings are used.
Accountability and learning are assumed to be the same thing
Most frameworks treat reporting to funders and learning for practice as naturally compatible. They are not. The tension between them is real, and it needs deliberate design — not wishful thinking.
Reports are built to be filed, not to be used
Evidence usability is an afterthought. Community feedback products, if they exist at all, are translations of technical documents. Nobody asks at the design stage: who actually needs to use this, and how?
Uncomfortable findings get smoothed over
Systems built to surface positive results are not trustworthy. The pressure to show success — to funders, to boards, to partners — quietly shapes what gets measured, reported, and remembered.
